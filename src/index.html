Post ideas — 10 posts per week
Moment first, always. 3 types: Buyer, Recipient Joy, Brand. 2 posts feature Rosie. Describe the situation, occasion, or feeling to a friend. Never sell. Each post ends with a single aim sentence.
For the Buyer
3 posts · the person searching, worrying, choosing
Post 1 — Late on a Wednesday
BUYER
It's gone 11. You've got seventeen browser tabs open, a birthday this Friday, and you've already had to close four websites because none of them are actually suitable for someone who is gluten free.

Not "mostly fine." Not "check the labels." You need something where the whole thing is right, without you having to investigate it at midnight.

Every product in every hamper here is plant-based, gluten free, and dairy free. Not a filter option. That is just what everything is.
Visual: laptop screen at night, soft glow, search bar visible · Tone: knowing, understated · Aim: show the buyer that their specific late-night anxiety is already solved.
Post 2 — The group WhatsApp
BUYER
Someone in the group chat mentioned a birthday next week and then added "she's dairy free and her husband is coeliac, so just so you know." Everyone went a bit quiet.

You know that feeling. Not impossible, just suddenly more complicated than it needs to be. Everyone second-guessing whether their idea is still appropriate.

A hamper that works for both of them, the way a thoughtful gift should, without it becoming a project.
Visual: phone with group chat notification, blurred · Tone: familiar, warm, not loud · Aim: reach the person managing a group gift where dietary needs made it awkward.
Post 3 — The first time
BUYER
She was diagnosed last autumn. Coeliac. Suddenly every food gift feels like a gamble, and you are the one holding the responsibility for whether she spends the next three days feeling unwell.

You have done the research. You have read the ingredients on things you would never normally read. You are being incredibly careful, and also slightly exhausted by how much effort it takes just to be kind.

This is exactly the situation these hampers are made for.
Visual: wrapped hamper, clean and considered · Tone: serious but warm, no drama · Aim: speak to someone buying for a newly diagnosed person for the first time.
Recipient Joy
3 posts · the moment of receiving · 1 feat. Rosie
Post 4 — The voice note at 8am
RECIPIENT
Some gifts get a polite thank you message later that week. Some gifts get a voice note at 8am the day they arrive, still half-unwrapped, just because she could not wait.

The difference is not the price. The difference is whether the whole thing felt like it was chosen for her specifically, not just ordered and crossed off a list.

The 8am message. That is what people are actually after when they start searching.
Visual: phone with voice message bubble, morning light · Tone: quiet, observational · Aim: help the buyer picture the exact reaction they want to create.
Post 5 — For a change
RECIPIENT
A customer left a review last week. I have been thinking about four words in it ever since.

"For a change, I got to eat everything."

For a change. She has spent years opening gifts and quietly setting aside the things she cannot have. Watching other people get through the good bits. This time was different. That is the whole point of this.
Visual: hamper contents fully laid out, abundant, nothing missing · Tone: still, deliberate, let the quote land · Aim: show the emotional significance of a gift where nothing needs to be set aside.
Post 6 — Rosie opens one
RECIPIENT ROSIE
I pack these. I know what goes in every single layer. And I still catch myself properly smiling when I open one.

The caramel truffle in the Signature right now took six months to find. Not because the alternatives were difficult to source. Because most of them tasted fine, and fine is not what this is for.

I go through the whole hamper on camera. Everything in it, why it is there, and whether I would put it in front of my mum without a second thought. [video]
Visual: Rosie, casual, natural light, not styled · Tone: genuine, specific, first person · Aim: let Rosie's standards speak for the brand without the brand speaking about itself.
Brand Building
4 posts · who we are and how we think · 2 feat. Rosie
Post 7 — The text I used to have to send
BRAND ROSIE
My friend Sarah is coeliac. For years, sending her any kind of food gift meant also sending a message that said something like "just a heads up, the two in the middle are probably fine but double check."

I stopped sending that message when I started making these. Not because I stopped caring. Because I no longer needed to.

Everything in every hamper is something I could send her, no caveats, no follow-up text, nothing to check. That is still the test every product has to pass.
Visual: Rosie, candid, not posed · Tone: personal, origin, specific name, no brand speech · Aim: connect the founder's personal reason to the product standard, without making a claim.
Post 8 — The one we said no to
BRAND ROSIE
We had a supplier last month that was genuinely lovely. Good story, beautiful packaging, tasted exactly right in sampling.

Then I read the full ingredients. One line. Not "may contain." Actually in it. That was the end of that conversation.

The supplier seemed surprised. Apparently most people do not read that far. We do, every time, for every product, and the answer has to be the same every time.
Visual: sample products on a table, ingredient label in focus · Tone: matter-of-fact, no drama, understated standards · Aim: show the rigour of product selection through a real decision, not a claim.
Post 9 — Sunday, half a podcast in
BRAND BUILD
Tissue paper everywhere. The podcast is on episode two. Someone ordered this one at 11pm last night for a birthday tomorrow afternoon.

There is a particular way these go together. The tissue first, then the heavier products, then the things that need to sit on top in a specific order so that the first thing she sees when the lid comes off is the right thing.

Nobody is watching when we pack these. They all go out the same way regardless.
Visual: packing bench, tissue paper, natural light, no styling · Tone: quiet, behind the scenes, no performance · Aim: show care through process without naming care as a value.
Post 10 — What "may contain" actually means
BRAND BUILD
"May contain gluten" means it was made in the same facility as something that contains gluten. Which is completely legal and fairly standard in food production. It also means that for someone who is coeliac, the product is not suitable.

A lot of gifts labelled as gluten free contain products with exactly this wording. The label is technically correct. The gift is still not safe for the person it was bought for.

Every product in these hampers has been checked for this specifically. That is one of the things that takes the time it takes.
Visual: ingredient label close-up, clean · Tone: informative, calm, no alarm · Aim: educate buyers on the difference between gluten free and truly safe, building quiet authority.

Social platform performance — Dream Value analysis
Rate any post across the four Dream Value axes to understand why it performed. High scorers become creative briefs for Edith.
Dream Value Equation — content framework
Score = (Dream × Likelihood) ÷ (Time × Effort)
Higher score = more compelling post. Maximise the top (aspiration + believability), minimise the bottom (instant + frictionless). Every post Edith creates should push towards the top-right quadrant: high dream, high belief, instant, zero effort.
Dream Outcome
Show the deeply desirable result. The 8am text. The recipient's face. The gift landing perfectly for someone who never expects to be included.
EDEN post ideas
  • Rosie reading a review: "she texted me at 8am"
  • Unboxing — recipient's reaction, not product close-up
  • "For a change, I got to eat everything" — real review, let it breathe
Perceived Likelihood
Make it feel achievable. Real proof. Real names. "I've done this myself." Social proof from people who look like the buyer — not brand polish.
EDEN post ideas
  • Named customer review — Adrienne, real quote, real stars
  • Rosie: "I ordered one for my mum last week. This is what she said."
  • Before/after: "I was the person reading labels at midnight. Here's what I do now."
Time Delay
Lower = better. Remove the gap between seeing the post and imagining receiving the gift. Instant satisfaction. "Order tonight, delivered tomorrow."
EDEN post ideas
  • "Order before 2pm — out today." Next-day framing in caption or CTA
  • Packing bench shot: "This one goes out in an hour"
  • Before/after same day: ordered at 9am, unboxed at 3pm
Effort & Sacrifice

Key terms index — EDEN & CO. complete reference
Advertising & PPC
ROAS
Return on Ad Spend. Revenue ÷ ad spend. Google target 3x+. Amazon/Meta target 5x+. Blended target 4x+.
Blended ROAS
All paid channels combined. Total revenue attributable to ads ÷ total ad spend across Google, Amazon, and Meta.
TACOS
Total Advertising Cost of Sale. Total ad spend ÷ total revenue (all channels, not just ad-attributed). Target below 15%. Best read across all channels simultaneously.
ACOS
Advertising Cost of Sale. Amazon-specific metric. Ad spend ÷ ad-attributed revenue on Amazon only. Target below 25%.
CPA
Cost Per Acquisition. Ad spend ÷ number of orders. Google ceiling: £15.90 blended. By SKU: Petite £3.25, Cocoa £6.25, Signature £10.91, Pamper £11.62, Grand £19.00, Prestige £27.88.
PMAX
Performance Max. Google Ads campaign type that serves across all Google surfaces (Search, Shopping, Display, YouTube, Gmail, Maps) from a single campaign. Asset-based. EDEN & CO. PMAX runs at £45/day focused on letterbox gifts and select chocolate/signature hampers.
tROAS
Target ROAS. Google Smart Bidding strategy that sets bids to achieve a specified return on ad spend. EDEN & CO. Search campaigns reduced from 300% to 200% tROAS on 6 March 2026 to expand reach while maintaining margin.
Commercial & Financial
AOV
Average Order Value. Total revenue ÷ total orders. Target £50. Current blended forecast: £54.57. Key lever: upsell from Letterbox to Signature.
Contribution Margin
Revenue minus COGS, platform commission, and ad spend. Target 45–50%. Shopify is the only full-margin channel at ~2.5% commission. Amazon is double-hit: 15% commission + PPC costs.
Min Viable Price
COGS ÷ (1 – commission%). Lowest price that covers costs on a given platform before a single penny of profit. Shopify Petite: £12.82. Amazon Petite: £14.71.
COGS
Cost of Goods Sold. Direct cost of making and packing one unit. Includes all BOM components. Petite ~£12.50, Cocoa ~£20.00, Signature ~£23.21, Pamper ~£33.00, Grand ~£50.00, Prestige ~£65.00.
BOM
Bill of Materials. Full itemised list of every component in a hamper — each product, packaging, tissue, ribbon, card — with unit cost. Used to calculate accurate COGS per SKU.
OpEx
Operating Expenditure. Fixed monthly overhead excluding COGS and ad spend. EDEN & CO. monthly OpEx: £10,230. Revenue must clear OpEx before any retained profit.
Run Rate
Annualised revenue projection based on current trading pace. Calculated as (revenue to date ÷ days elapsed) × 365. Useful as directional indicator but overstates Q4 impact when measured mid-year.
Attach Rate
Percentage of orders that include an add-on product (greeting card, candle, personalisation). Each 1pp increase in attach rate adds approximately £0.75 to blended AOV. Data currently partial — derived from SKU suffix in ShipStation.
Product & Stock
SKU
Stock Keeping Unit. Unique identifier for each product variant. Used in ShipStation, Amazon Seller Central, and Shopify to track inventory, fulfilment, and ad performance by product line.
Logistics & Shipping
DDP
Delivered Duty Paid. Shipping term meaning the seller covers all duties, taxes, and import fees. What the customer pays at checkout is the final price — no customs charges on delivery.
DDU
Delivered Duty Unpaid. The recipient pays import duties and fees on arrival. EDEN & CO. avoids DDU for consumer orders — it creates a bad unboxing experience and damages brand trust.
IoR
Importer of Record. The entity legally responsible for a shipment entering a country. For EU shipments: EDEN & CO. acts as IoR to ensure DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) — customer pays no import fees at the door.
EORI
Economic Operators Registration and Identification number. Required for any business exporting from or importing into the UK and EU. Must be quoted on commercial invoices for all cross-border shipments.
IOSS
Import One-Stop Shop. EU VAT scheme for goods sold to EU consumers where the total value is under €150. EDEN & CO. collects and remits EU VAT at point of sale. Eliminates surprise charges for the recipient.
WorldEase
UPS cross-border solution for DDP shipping to EU and US. Recommended long-term international carrier for EDEN & CO. Currently in UPS setup phase (March 2026).
Dietary & Allergens
Plant-Based
EDEN & CO. preferred term for vegan products. Framing: abundant and delicious, not restrictive. "Plant-based" avoids the health-food connotation of "vegan" and sits better in a premium gifting context.
Vegan
Contains no animal-derived ingredients including dairy, eggs, honey, gelatin, carmine, and beeswax. All EDEN & CO. products are vegan. Brand language: "plant-based", not "vegan" as the primary lead (dietary is always sixth in the messaging hierarchy).
Gluten Free
Contains no gluten-containing grains (wheat, barley, rye, oats unless certified GF). EDEN & CO. products are made without gluten ingredients. Important caveat: "may contain" labelling due to shared production facilities is a known customer concern flagged in reviews — watch area for supplier audits.
Dairy Free
Contains no milk, cream, butter, cheese, lactose, or other dairy derivatives. 32.9% of EDEN & CO. buyers specify dairy free as a requirement. Often overlaps with vegan but not always — some customers are dairy free but not fully vegan.
Coeliac
Autoimmune condition triggered by gluten ingestion. Stricter than "gluten free preference" — cross-contamination risk matters. Coeliac customers scrutinise "may contain" warnings closely. Mentioned in 6 of 673 Google reviews. High-trust segment: a coeliac who trusts EDEN & CO. becomes a loyal repeat buyer.
